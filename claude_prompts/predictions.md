#### Claude's scoping notes & questions — Migration prompt 1 (2026-07-11)

Before predicting anything, here is my read of the terrain, the honest
uncertainty assessment you asked for, and seven scope questions. Nothing below

**A. Why caution is earned, not performative.** Climate-migration forecasting
has one of the worst public track records in the field. The canonical failure:
the 2005 UNEP-affiliated claim of **"50 million environmental refugees by
2010"** (echoing Norman Myers' numbers) — it did not happen and was quietly
retracted. The current literature spans **two orders of magnitude**: the World
Bank *Groundswell* models project up to **216 million *internal* climate
migrants by 2050** (and say concerted action could cut that ~80%); Xu et al.
(2020, PNAS) is quoted as "**1–3 billion** people left outside the historical
human climate niche by 2070" — but the authors themselves state it "cannot be
interpreted as a prediction of migration"; low-end econometric estimates for
*cross-border* climate-driven movement run to only tens of millions by 2100.
Any prediction we publish must be located inside this spread, with its
conditioning assumptions visible (Principles 1 and 5).

**B. What is actually well-established (the base to build on).**
- Today's baselines: ~**281–304 million international migrants (~3.6% of
  humanity)** — a share that has risen only slowly for decades; **83.4 million
  internally displaced at end-2024** (IDMC), of whom most are displaced by
  *conflict*; a record **45.8 million new disaster displacements in 2024** —
  but most disaster displacement is *internal, short-distance, and temporary*.
- The strongest empirical regularities: climate stress mostly produces
  **internal and regional** movement, not intercontinental flows; migration is
  **mixed-motive** (climate acts through crops, income, conflict risk — rarely
  as a clean single cause); and the poorest are often **"trapped populations"**
  — severe impacts can *reduce* out-migration by destroying the resources
  needed to move. Destination **policy**, not source-region push, is the
  dominant control on international numbers.
- There is **no legal category of "climate refugee"** (a gap in the 1951
  Convention; the 2020 *Teitiota* UN HRC decision opened a crack). Predictions
  about "refugee flows" are therefore partly predictions about future *law*.

**C. The main uncertainties, ranked as I see them.**
1. **Adaptation in place** (largest): whether people build seawalls, switch
   crops, install AC, or leave. Historically, staying-and-adapting dominates.
2. **Destination policy**: border regimes and labor demand in aging rich
   countries (our §12 aging numbers cut *toward* more wanted migration even as
   politics cuts against — a genuine two-sided uncertainty).
3. **Climate scenario**: current-policy trajectories (~2.5–3 °C by 2100) vs
   tail risks; sea-level commitment matters most after 2050.
4. **Attribution/definition**: what we count as "climate migration" can swing
   the number by 10×; we must fix definitions before quoting numbers.
5. **Conflict coupling**: the largest displacement flows today are war-driven;
   climate–conflict links are real but weak-to-moderate in the literature.

**Answering Q2 — the evidence on internal vs. international, and *why* people
move.** You were right not to accept 70/30 on my say-so. Here is the basis,
global then Brazil-specific:

- *Why people move (the mechanism).* Migration is overwhelmingly **economic and
  mixed-motive**; climate acts as a **"threat multiplier"** through income, not
  as a standalone push. The best-identified econometrics (Cattaneo & Peri 2016)
  find an **inverted-U**: in poor agrarian economies, warming that cuts farm
  income *reduces* emigration (people are trapped — no capital to move), while
  in middle-income economies it raises rural-to-urban movement. Brazil today
  sits at the upper-middle-income end, which matters for which arm of the U it
  is on.
- *Why mostly internal.* Moving costs money, networks, and legal access;
  crossing borders costs far more of each. Globally, IDMC disaster displacement
  (45.8 M new in 2024) is **overwhelmingly internal and often temporary**; the
  World Bank *Groundswell* projections (up to 216 M by 2050) are **explicitly
  internal-only by construction**. International climate-attributable flows are
  an order of magnitude smaller in the credible literature.
- *Brazil's own history confirms the pattern.* The defining 20th-century flow
  was **internal and drought-linked**: *nordestinos* leaving the semi-arid
  Northeast *sertão* for São Paulo and the Southeast — millions over decades,
  climate (drought) acting through rural poverty. The template for Brazil's
  climate future is far more likely to be Northeast→Southeast/urban again than
  Brazil→abroad.
- *Brazil is now a net receiver, which cuts against a big emigration story.* By
  end-2024 Brazil hosted **>800,000 refugees/asylum-seekers** (Venezuelans
  ~474k, Haitians, Cubans, Angolans) under a notably liberal regime (2017
  Migration Law, *Operação Acolhida*). Brazilian emigration to the US/Portugal
  is real but driven by economy/inequality, not yet climate.
- *So my proposed weighting for Brazil:* lead with **internal** (Northeast
  semi-arid and Amazon/North → Southeast and South cities; plus intra-urban
  displacement from floods like Porto Alegre 2024, ~580k displaced), treat
  **international** as a secondary channel in *both* directions (emigration
  pressure *and* Brazil as a regional climate haven for Andean/Caribbean
  neighbors). I'll show the evidence in the piece rather than assert a ratio.
  **Does that framing match your intent?**
>A. Yes, that framing matches our intent.
